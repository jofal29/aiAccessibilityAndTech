Accessibility in AI: Speech Recognition & Neurodiversity

Image source: LinkedIn Article by Xun Qian



Using AI’s speech recognition can completely transform accessibility. Think voice assistants, dictation tools, and real-time transcription to aid users in their day-to-day lives. However, for neurodiverse users-like those with autism, speech processing disorders, or ADHD-these tools can be more frustrating than helpful, sometimes causing overstimulation.

Throughout this semester, we learned that true accessibility in technology requires not only technical solutions but also a shift in perspective-moving from designing for the “average” user to embracing the full spectrum of human diversity. This means recognizing that neurodivergent users, with their unique ways of communicating and processing information, must be actively included in both the data that trains AI and the teams that build it. By applying these inclusive design principles, we ensure that AI-driven tools genuinely empower all users, rather than reinforcing existing barriers.

Neurodiverse users often speak with atypical rhythms, inflections, and phrasing. When AI models are trained mostly on “neurotypical” speech, they may misinterpret neurodivergent users’ words and input, leading to incorrect responses or repeated failures. In a world where tech meant to assist ends up alienating, we’ve failed at inclusive design.

For example, someone with echolalia-a condition often seen in autism where phrases are repeated out of context-may be misunderstood by AI, causing the system to get stuck in loops and create confusion instead of support. Users with ADHD may speak quickly or change topics fast, and speech models often struggle to keep up or maintain context.

Applying a virtue ethics lens helps here. Instead of only asking what maximizes utility (like utilitarianism) or what rule is being followed (like deontology), we should focus on character and compassion. An ethical technologist should strive to build systems structured in patience, empathy, and adaptability-not perfection.

Real systems like Google’s Project Euphonia are beginning to address this gap by training models on diverse voice patterns, but this needs to become a standard, not a one-off experiment.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES: ACCESSIBILITY IN AI

Explore case studies that show how AI-powered tools and inclusive design can transform accessibility for neurodiverse users. These real-world examples highlight both the challenges and successes in making technology more inclusive.

Google: Project Euphonia

Many people with speech impairments or atypical speech patterns-such as those with ALS, Down syndrome, or autism-struggle to use mainstream speech recognition tools, which are typically trained on neurotypical voices.

How AI/Assistive Tech Was Used: Google’s Project Euphonia uses deep learning to train speech recognition models on thousands of voice samples from people with diverse speech patterns. By including these voices, the models become better at recognizing and transcribing atypical speech.

Impact/Outcome: Project Euphonia has made it possible for people with speech impairments to use voice assistants and dictation software more reliably, increasing independence and digital access. It also sets a new standard for inclusive AI design by involving users with disabilities in the development process.

Microsoft: Immersive Reader

Students with dyslexia and other reading disabilities often struggle to access and comprehend written material, limiting their participation in mainstream classrooms.

How AI/Assistive Tech Was Used: Microsoft’s Immersive Reader uses AI to read text aloud, break words into syllables, and adjust text spacing and background color. These features help users decode and process text more easily.

Impact/Outcome: Immersive Reader has improved reading comprehension and engagement for students with dyslexia and other learning differences. It demonstrates how AI can be tailored to support neurodiverse learners and foster greater inclusion in education.

Case Study: Use of Generative AI to Help People with Disabilities

The University of Washington did a case study on Generative Artificial Intelligence(GAI) and its utility on accessibility. The team did the study had people of "mixed" abilities and of different minority groups.

They asked two questions: "Can Generative AI Help Meet Access Needs for People with Disabilities?" and "Can Generative AI Help Make Content Accessible for Others?" We will focus on the anecdote for the first question.

Story of Ally: Ally has a disability named aphantasia. This causes the person to be unable to visualize and form mental images of objects. Ally has trouble visualizing a fictional novel scene and thus relies on movie adaptations. However, using GAI helps her visualize the scenes right away.

Not only does Ally use GAI for her pastimes, but also for work. Ally works as a UX developer. However, her aphantasia and migraines affect her product design and development process, and so she uses GAI to see visuals.

Impact/Outcome: The study discusses that there were various examples of ableism in the stories shared and that they confirmed biases towards disabled people. Further, Generative AI would provide a false solution, meaning that the user still had to go back and fix these errors. However, the issue with that is the fact that GAI content could be used for material to help disabled people, but if they are not experienced in this area, these biases could go through.

Practical Takeaways


Recommendation Why It Matters
AI speech tools should be trained on a wider range of voices, including neurodiverse users. Ensures speech recognition works for everyone, not just neurotypical users.
Developmental teams must consult with neurodivergent individuals during design. Direct feedback leads to more inclusive and effective solutions.
Encourage participatory design by involving neurodivergent users in user testing and feedback cycles. Identifies real-world challenges and usability issues early.
Accessibility isn’t just about adding features; it’s about rethinking the default user model. Prevents exclusion by addressing systemic design biases.
Regularly audit AI systems for unintended bias and update models as new patterns of use emerge. Keeps technology fair and adaptive to changing needs.
Provide customizable user settings that allow individuals to tailor AI interactions to their sensory and communication preferences. Empowers users to adjust tools for their unique needs.
Question to ask: “Who is being left out by this design, and how do we center them instead?” Keeps the focus on inclusion throughout the development process.
